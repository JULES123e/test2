OilX Opinion

16 june 2022

Oil Awash In Mixed signals

• Strong crude pricing not matched by recent inventory changes
• Localised crude balances and more bullish consensus on still large uncertainties driving pricing
• Incoming product demand data already suggest a softening in implied crude needs even this summer, let alone next year


Physical crude and futures pricing is rapidly approaching the strength observed immediately after the outbreak of conflict in Ukraine. This is somewhat at odds with the global crude balance, which is still trending above seasonal averages, builds in commercial crude inventory since January, and reduced uncertainties regarding Russian oil exports for the short term.
Brent futures have been sustaining extreme backwardation once again, while cash/futures spreads and diffs in West Africa and the Med have been on a tear. M6 Brent remains at some $110. Net length in Brent has risen considerably over the last few weeks. All this, while Russian crude exports are trending at some of the highest levels seen in more than two years and significantly stronger than pre-conflict. Russian product exports are also relatively stable, while refinery intake and crude production gained significantly over H1 June.
It is reasonable to say that in this extremely difficult market, fair value for timespreads, relative prices, and margins remains extremely difficult to assess. Reading too much into large moves currently is somewhat futile. However, we think some price signals and their notional market messages do not quite match up with the current physical and marginal indications about the future. (Let us for the moment ignore significant tail-end risks, such as the unilateral and pre-emptive reduction in oil and gas to Europe from the Russian side. A quick look at European natgas these last few days is a reminder of such risks).

West Of Suez Perfect Storm

In the crude market, particularly West of Suez (WoS), an extreme price setup is being impacted by extra-ordinary as well as one-off factors. These include:

• Significant outages and maintenance, including maintenance in Norway and Kazakhstan, and outages in Libya
• The ongoing redirection of global crude flows - Europe is pulling in significantly more barrels from the Atlantic Basin (ex-Russia) than pre-conflict, alongside extra Middle Eastern barrels. Preference for light sweets has been high up to this point
• At the same time Europe had a very strong overall month of imports in May, which serviced higher runs but also built stocks
• Gasoline’s meteoric rise since April and massive residue sulphur spreads have also boosted light-sweet crude GPWs
• Brent futures/spreads are more prone to geopolitical and speculative influences than other benchmarks (more on this below)

Forward Expectations Turned More Bullish
While Macro Darkens

We are not heedless of the fact that forward expectations are also driving prices. The consensus appears to be trending if anything more bullish. We think this moves against the marginal indications of what balances will look like even for the next weeks, let alone the rest of the year and into 2023.
EU phase out was a key moment of the last few weeks, but widely anticipated in its eventual form. The phase out of non-exempted Russian oil should, however, take place relatively slowly. Our June nowcast for Russian flows to OECD Europe is actually higher m/m. A slow phase-out gives Russia ample time to explore the various options to keep its oil on the market.
The insurance ban included in the sixth round of sanctions may not ultimately prove a great hinderance to Russian liftings, even if the UK enacts its own version and assuming the US administration does not work to soften the measures. State-backed and off-circuit insurers, the utilisation of state-owned and a variety of smaller tanker players, the use of STS, and other regulatory loopholes all provide outlets. It may be more likely that freight markets simply increasingly bifurcate (with strong implications for rates in the main freight tier).
OPEC production rightfully remains a key source of speculation and invariably a bullish one particularly when viewed through the by now rather defunct lens of missed OPEC+ targets. We think Saudi, Kuwait, UAE, and Iraq still have at least some upside through to September. Beyond that, things get difficult - political pressure may see some of them break through capacity barriers by drawing stocks for some weeks, but obviously this is not a medium-term solution.
Talk of flatlining crude exports also often does not account for rising crude runs – June throughput in these four countries is running a good 700 KBD higher y/y, and with further upside ahead given the start-up of Al Zour (Jazan also has the potential to run higher). That reduces crude exports but also increases much-needed product exports.
China has recently also invariably been painted in a bullish light for the rest of the year, despite the doubling down of authorities on ‘Zero Covid’ and renewed outbreaks of an extremely transmissible virus. What is more, China is already importing crude at levels higher than last year – so in order to view China as a bullish element in the coming quarters (i.e. even higher imports), one needs to predict no further lockdowns, a return to normal crude runs, and a continuation of strong stock builds as well (Apr-May builds ran at +850 KBD – we presume this won’t be sustained in any event).
Refinery runs (ex-China) continue to struggle higher. OECD runs could still have significant upside in our view but may struggle to maintain much higher levels than now for several months at a time. That means our already manageable summer crude balances get softer but it also paves the way for further volatility in margins and cracks. The US is also approaching Hurricane Season. As such and with little progress having been made on rebuilding product stocks in recent weeks, Q3 or Q4 margins remain potential targets to be dragged up by prompt disruptions.
Product demand is not liable to shift that much from consensus in the short term, though there is an argument that the most difficult areas to monitor demand are also the most likely to witness stronger demand destruction (the Non-OECD, where economic/social crises continue to spring up). Even in the US, weekly gasoline and diesel numbers are combined painting a picture that is difficult to ignore and very soft relative to expectations and seasonality.
What about the golden child of demand this year – jet? NWE jet is soaring for now, but outside of this region, dare we say it that demand (ex-Russia and China) is actually disappointing? It is early days yet, but our June nowcasts are not yet showing the type of seasonal uptick that might be expected or was seen even last year. Progress is slow in many formerly important jet demand nations particularly East of Suez, such as Thailand and Hong Kong. Overall, progress against pre-Covid levels also appears to have stalled.
demand is coming into the spotlight and is still viewed with a lot of upside, driven structurally by post-Covid recoveries. There are growing signals that even if demand this summer to a great extent meets expectations, even rich nations are already running on fumes in terms of their ability to manage these high prices and the rest of their cost-of-living issues (including strongly rising mortgage repayments etc.). Spiking consumer credit data reflects this notion. That could imply a post-summer slump.
The IEA has now had its first crack at 2023 demand growth, forecasting a huge 2.2 MBD y/y on top of 1.8 MBD in 2022. Have prolonged high prices, a widespread cost-of-living crisis, likely recession, spreading social and economic disruption, and structural behavioural changes been addressed in full yet? Big growth numbers are also dependent on China emerging from this and from covid unscathed, and therefore rapidly accelerating its oil demand numbers next year. Meanwhile, the EIA has lowered its outright 2023 oil demand levels by about 1 million b/d since January, with both 2022 and 2023 growth revised incrementally lower. The similarities to forecasting efforts between 2007 and 2009 are already there, but the current bull cycle thesis is built upon an increasingly rosy end-user demand basis. The way things are headed, there is an argument to be made that demand growth next year can, at best, be limited to as much supply growth can be put together.
